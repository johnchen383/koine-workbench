title: The hardest ἵνα in Mark — purpose, result, or citation formula?
tags: hina, purpose-clause, ot-quotation
---
Jesus explains the parables: to those outside everything happens in parables, **ἵνα** "seeing they may see and not perceive… *lest* they turn and be forgiven" (quoting Isaiah 6:9–10).

Taken as a full **purpose** clause ("*in order that* they may not perceive"), Jesus speaks in parables *to prevent* understanding — theologically hard. The main alternatives:

- **Purpose** (face value): parables conceal as judgment — matches Isaiah 6's commission of judicial hardening, and Mark's insider/outsider theme (4:11).
- **Result**: "*with the result that* they see but don't perceive" — grammatically possible in Koine, softer.
- **Citation shorthand**: ἵνα introduces the Isaiah quote almost like "so that [the scripture is fulfilled which says]…" — compare Matthew 13:13, which changes it to ὅτι ("because they do not see"), visibly softening Mark.

Note μήποτε at the end: "lest" (purpose-negative) or "unless perhaps" (leaving a door open — some rabbinic readings of Isaiah took it that way). Every English version of this verse is a theology-laden decision; there is no neutral rendering.
